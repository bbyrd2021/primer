Primer is a conversational research intelligence tool. Researchers upload their literature — up to 50 PDFs — state a research question, and get a grounded assistant that knows only those papers. Every answer cites a real source. Nothing is invented. It is the pre-writing layer that no existing tool covers.

01 — Vision & Positioning

One-line pitch

Primer is the research assistant that only knows what you've read — and proves it.

Product vision

In five years, every serious researcher has a Primer project for every paper they write. It holds their literature, surfaces what they've missed, tells them where the gaps are, and helps them walk into any writing tool with a structured foundation. It never hallucinates. It never forgets. It costs less than a textbook per month.

Market position

The gap Primer fills: Elicit and Consensus help you find papers. Zotero helps you manage them. Prism and Jenni help you write. Nobody owns the stage in between — the messy, hours-long process of reading, synthesizing, and making sense of 30–50 papers before a single word of the lit review is written. That's Primer's territory.

ChatGPT / Claude.ai Answers research questions Halluccinates citations, no persistent library

Honest differentiation from NotebookLM: Google NotebookLM is the closest surface-level competitor. The difference is depth of research-specific features: structured paper card extraction, relevance scoring, gap identification, citation-first grounding, and CSV/BibTeX export. NotebookLM is a general document chat tool. Primer is built for one workflow.

02 — Problem Statement

The problem in one sentence

Researchers doing literature reviews spend the majority of their pre-writing time switching between disconnected tools, losing context between sessions, and manually extracting information from papers they've already read.

The four specific pain points Primer addresses

Pain 01 — Tool fragmentation

The average lit review involves 5–7 separate tools: a search tool (Elicit), a reference manager (Zotero), a PDF reader (Preview or Adobe), a notes tool (Notion or Obsidian), an AI chat (ChatGPT), a writing tool (Google Docs or Word), and a citation formatter. Each context switch costs cognitive overhead and lost state.

Pain 02 — No persistent research context

Every session in ChatGPT, Claude.ai, or any LLM chat starts from zero. The researcher must re-explain their project, re-upload papers, and rebuild context from scratch. Work done in yesterday's session is inaccessible today.

Pain 03 — Hallucinated citations

General-purpose LLMs fabricate citations that sound plausible. Researchers who catch this must verify every claim manually — which defeats the purpose of using AI assistance. Those who don't catch it face academic integrity risk.

Pain 04 — Manual paper extraction

Researchers maintain manual spreadsheets (like the one Primer was built from) tracking methodology, results, datasets, and relevance scores per paper. This is hours of work that could be automated if the system read the actual PDF.

Origin story: Primer was built because its founder maintained a 20+ row spreadsheet tracking BDD100K and nuScenes perception papers by hand — methodology, results, dataset, code links, personal notes, relevance tier. That spreadsheet is the product's north star. Every field in Primer's paper card maps directly to a column in that spreadsheet.

04 — Core Concept

The mental model

Primer is a research assistant that only knows what you've given it. This constraint is not a limitation — it is the product's primary trust mechanism. When Primer tells you something, it can only be from a paper you uploaded. The citation is always real. The source always exists. The page number always checks out.

The closest analogy: imagine you could hire a brilliant research assistant, give them your entire paper stack, and ask them anything — but they're not allowed to go to the internet or use any outside knowledge. That assistant would be Primer.

The two output modes

Every Primer project produces output in two complementary forms:

Mode A — Paper Cards (structured extraction)

For each uploaded PDF, Primer extracts a structured card: title, venue, methodology, key results, datasets, code availability, and a relevance score against the user's research question. Cards are displayed as a sortable table and exportable as CSV. This replaces the manual spreadsheet.

Mode B — Conversational Brief (RAG chat)

The user chats with their paper library in natural language. Every response is grounded — meaning every claim cites the specific paper and page number it comes from. The conversation can produce a structured brief (synthesis, debates, gaps, outline) or answer ad-hoc questions ("what did Chen et al. say about anchor-free detection?").

Why chat, not just a one-shot brief? Research questions evolve mid-review. A researcher might start asking about "multi-task detection architectures" and realize mid-conversation that the real gap is in "real-time embedded inference." One-shot briefs force premature question commitment. Chat lets the research thinking develop naturally — which is how it actually works.

07 — Chat Interface Specification

UI layout

Three-panel layout on desktop: left sidebar (projects), center panel (chat), right panel (paper cards). On mobile: single column with tab navigation between chat and cards.

Grounding behavior

Every AI response must follow this citation format: after each claim, an inline citation appears as [Author et al., p.X]. Citations are rendered as clickable chips. Clicking a chip opens the source chunk in a side drawer showing the exact extracted text.

When Primer doesn't know

If the retrieval system finds no relevant chunks, Primer must respond: "I don't see anything in your uploaded papers that addresses this. You may want to search for additional literature on this topic." It must never speculate beyond the provided literature. This is a hard behavioral rule enforced by the system prompt, not a suggestion.

Sample interaction

Project: AV Perception Survey · 18 papers indexed
Grounded mode · Citations only from uploaded papers
What are the main architectural approaches to multi-task driving perception, and where do they disagree?
There are two dominant architectural philosophies in your uploaded literature. The first uses a shared encoder with task-specific decoder heads — this approach is used by both YOLOP and HybridNets, which use a single backbone to extract features then branch into separate heads for detection, driveable area segmentation, and lane detection.
↳ YOLOPX_2024.pdf, p.3 · HybridNets_2022.pdf, p.4

The key disagreement is around anchor-based vs. anchor-free detection heads. HybridNets uses anchor-based detection with k-means clustering for prior boxes, while YOLOPX explicitly argues this is a limitation and uses a decoupled anchor-free head from YOLOX for improved flexibility.
↳ YOLOPX_2024.pdf, p.5 · HybridNets_2022.pdf, p.6

A secondary disagreement involves training strategy: HybridNets uses a staged multi-phase training to reduce compute, while YOLOPX uses SimOTA label assignment and argues this achieves better performance without multi-stage complexity.
↳ YOLOPX_2024.pdf, p.7
Generate my research brief
Generating your research brief from all 18 indexed papers... (structured brief with Synthesis, Debates, Gaps, and Outline sections will appear here)
Ask anything about your papers...

System prompt architecture

The chat system prompt has three layers:

Layer 1 — Role: "You are a research assistant. You only have access to the source chunks provided to you. Your job is to help the researcher understand, synthesize, and navigate their literature."

Layer 2 — Grounding rule: "Every factual claim must cite its source chunk as [Filename, p.X]. If you cannot support a claim with a provided chunk, state that explicitly. Do not use outside knowledge."

Layer 3 — Project context: "The user's research question is: {research_question}. They have uploaded {n} papers. Prioritize responses relevant to this research question."

08 — Paper Card Specification

Extracted fields

interface PaperCard {
  title:           string;          // Full paper title as written
  authors:         string[];        // Author list, up to 5 then "et al."
  venue:           string;          // Conference or journal name (e.g. CVPR, Pattern Recognition)
  year:            number;          // Publication year as integer (e.g. 2024)
  task:            string;          // Primary ML/research task
  modality:        string;          // Camera, LiDAR, Camera+LiDAR, etc.
  methodology:     string;          // 1–2 sentence core contribution
  results:         string;          // Key metrics, copied verbatim from paper
  datasets:        string[];        // All datasets used in experiments
  pretraining:     string | null;   // Backbone/weight init, or null
  code_available:  boolean;         // True if repo URL found in paper
  code_url:        string | null;   // GitHub or project page URL
  key_limitations: string | null;   // Limitations stated by authors
  synthesis_note:  string;          // 2–3 sentences: relevance to research question
  relevance_score: 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5; // Scored against research question
  tier:            1 | 2 | 3;       // 1=must-read, 2=should-read, 3=background
  filename:        string;          // Original uploaded filename
  session_id:      string;          // Project/session UUID
  extracted_at:    string;          // ISO 8601 timestamp of extraction
}

Relevance scoring rubric

Score Tier Meaning
5 1 Directly addresses the research question. Core methodology overlap. Must-read.
4 1 Highly relevant. Significant overlap with question or methodology. Must-read.
3 2 Relevant dataset, partial methodology overlap, or important context. Should-read.
2 3 Loosely related. Background context. Useful for framing but not core.
1 3 Minimal relevance to research question. Tangential at best.
On extraction quality: Claude will not always get it right, especially for methodology and results fields on complex papers. The card is a starting point, not a replacement for reading. The manual edit feature (P2) exists precisely because researchers need to correct and enrich the auto-extracted content. Primer's value is getting them 80% of the way there in 2 minutes, not 100% of the way in 20.

09 — Research Brief Specification

The research brief is triggered by the user (via a button or by typing "generate my research brief" in chat). It runs a fresh RAG retrieval across all indexed papers with the research question, then produces a structured document.

Brief structure

Section Content Citation requirement
Synthesis Thematic summary of what the literature collectively says about the research question. Written as connected prose, not bullet points. Every sentence cites at least one source
Key Debates & Contradictions Where sources disagree with each other. Both sides cited. Both sides of each debate must be sourced
Research Gaps What the literature does NOT address relative to the research question. Based only on absence of coverage in uploaded papers. Must reference which papers were checked; cannot speculate
Suggested Outline 5–7 section outline for a literature review, organized by themes found in synthesis. Each section maps to at least one source paper

What the brief is NOT

The brief does not write the literature review. It produces the scaffold. The prose in the Synthesis section is meant to show the researcher what the argument looks like — not to be copy-pasted into their paper. Primer's job ends at the outline. Prism, Word, and LaTeX take it from there.

Scalability considerations

The MVP architecture is deliberately local-first — ChromaDB persists to disk, PDFs live in the filesystem, card JSONs are flat files. This is intentional: it ships fast and costs nothing to run. The path to scale is clear: ChromaDB → Pinecone or pgvector, local files → S3, card JSONs → Supabase table. None of these migrations break the API contract.

13 — Grounding System

Citation grounding is Primer's primary trust differentiator. This section defines the exact enforcement mechanism.

The grounding contract

Primer makes one unconditional promise: every factual claim in a Primer response cites the specific paper and page number it came from. If Primer cannot support a claim with a real retrieved chunk, it must say so. This is enforced at the prompt level, not the hope level.

How it works mechanically

  1. User sends a message to the chat interface.
  2. The message is embedded and used to retrieve the top 15 most semantically relevant chunks from the project's ChromaDB collection.
  3. Retrieved chunks are formatted with their source metadata (filename + page number) and injected into the Claude API prompt.
  4. The system prompt instructs Claude to cite every claim using [Filename, p.X] format, and to explicitly say "not addressed in the provided literature" for any question the chunks don't answer.
  5. Claude's response is parsed to extract citation references and validate they match the retrieved chunk metadata before being returned to the user.

Failure modes and mitigations

Failure mode Likelihood Mitigation
Claude cites a real filename but wrong page number MED Validate cited page numbers against retrieved chunk metadata before returning response. Flag mismatches.
Claude paraphrases a chunk loosely and attribution is unclear MED Prompt instructs Claude to quote key phrases verbatim when citing, not just paraphrase. Source preview feature lets user verify.
Retrieved chunks are irrelevant (poor embedding quality) LOW all-MiniLM-L6-v2 is well-tested for semantic similarity. Increase n_results to 20 for complex questions.
User asks a question with no relevant chunks in their library LOW Primer must respond with "I don't see this addressed in your uploaded papers." Hard rule in system prompt.
Scanned PDF produces garbled text from OCR MED Detect low text-per-page ratio after extraction. Warn user: "This paper may be scanned — extraction quality may be low."

18 — Non-Goals

These are explicitly out of scope for MVP and v2. Saying no to these is how Primer ships.

  • Paper discovery. Primer does not search for papers. Users bring their own. Discovery is Elicit's job.
  • Prose writing assistance. Primer produces outlines and briefs, not finished prose. Writing is Prism's and Jenni's job.
  • Real-time collaboration. No live co-editing. No multiplayer. One researcher per project in MVP.
  • Mobile app. Web-responsive only. A native iOS or Android app is a v3+ consideration.
  • LaTeX integration. Primer is tool-agnostic for writing. BibTeX export covers the most important LaTeX need.
  • Training on user data. Uploaded PDFs are processed and stored per-user. They are never used to train or fine-tune any model. This is a permanent policy, not a temporary one.
  • Reference management (Zotero replacement). Primer extracts metadata but is not a full citation manager. Deep integration with Zotero is a later consideration.
  • General-purpose document chat. Primer is for academic research papers. It is not a general "chat with your PDFs" tool. This focus is the product.

19 — Open Questions

  1. How should Primer handle papers where full text extraction produces garbage? (Scanned PDFs, papers with complex multi-column layouts, papers with mostly figures.)
    Current plan: warn user, extract what we can, flag the card as "low confidence." OCR as v2 feature.
  2. Should the research question be editable after papers are uploaded?
    Yes (P2 feature) — triggers re-scoring of all cards. But this costs API calls. Should it be gated to Pro?
  3. What is the right chunk size for academic papers?
    Currently specced at 800 tokens with 100 overlap. Academic papers have dense, technical paragraphs. May need to be larger (1200) than typical RAG defaults. Needs empirical testing on real papers.
  4. How do we handle papers in languages other than English?
    Not addressed in MVP. Claude handles multilingual text — but the extraction prompt and card schema are English-optimized. Flag as known limitation.
  5. When does a free user get notified they're approaching limits?
    At 80% of limit (e.g., 24/30 chat messages used). Gives them a meaningful warning without immediately cutting them off. Converts anxiety into upgrade consideration.
  6. Should Primer offer a "team" or "lab" plan for research groups?
    Strong long-term signal. A PI who puts their whole lab on Primer at $49/month/lab is a more durable customer than individual Pro users. Deferred to v3 but worth early conversations with professors.
